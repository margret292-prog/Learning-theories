Theory 01 — Behaviorism

Learning is a change in observable behavior

Behaviorism focuses entirely on what can be seen and measured. Internal mental states are irrelevant — what matters is the connection between stimulus, response, and reinforcement.

Stimulus–Response
Conditioning
Reinforcement
Shaping
How does learning happen?

Learning occurs when a behavior becomes consistently linked to a stimulus through stimuli and responses. Positive outcomes (reinforcement) make behaviors more likely; negative outcomes (punishment) reduce them. Through repetition and feedback, new behaviors are shaped and maintained.

Stimulus
Input / trigger
→
Response
Observable behavior
→
Reinforcement ±
Outcome / consequence
Key Thinkers

Ivan Pavlov

Discovered classical conditioning through his famous dog experiments — pairing a neutral stimulus with food until the stimulus alone triggered salivation.

B.F. Skinner

Developed operant conditioning: behavior is shaped by its consequences. Positive reinforcement strengthens behavior; punishment weakens it.

John B. Watson

Founded behaviorism as a formal school. Argued psychology should focus only on observable behavior, not inner mental states.

Key Concepts
Classical Conditioning
Learning through repeated association of stimuli (Pavlov's dogs).
Operant Conditioning
Behavior shaped by rewards and punishments (Skinner).
Positive Reinforcement
Adding a reward to increase the likelihood of a behavior.
Negative Reinforcement
Removing an unpleasant stimulus to increase behavior.
Extinction
A behavior disappears when reinforcement is removed consistently.
Shaping
Reinforcing successive approximations to a target behavior.
Practical Tools for Your Classroom
🎯

Immediate, specific feedback

Reinforce desired behavior the moment it occurs. "Great — you followed the safety checklist correctly before starting the machine."

📋

Clear expectations & routines

Students learn faster when classroom procedures are explicitly stated and consistently applied. Post rules and follow them every time.

🔁

Drill & repetition exercises

Repetition builds automaticity. In vocational training, this means repeated practice of safety procedures, tool handling, or technical protocols.

📝

Behavior contracts

Co-create written agreements with students about expected conduct and consequences. Transparency reduces conflict.

✓ Strengths

Clear, measurable outcomes. Works well for procedural skills, safety training, and habit formation. Easy to apply consistently.

✗ Limitations

Ignores the inner life of the learner. Doesn't explain curiosity, intrinsic motivation, or how students solve novel problems they've never seen before.

✏ Reflect

How does behaviorism show up in your teaching?

Think about a recent lesson. Can you identify moments where you used reinforcement or set up clear stimulus–response patterns? What worked — and what felt mechanical?

Theory 02 — Constructivism

Learners build their own understanding

Constructivism holds that knowledge is not transferred from teacher to student — it is actively constructed by the learner through experience, exploration, and reflection.

Schema
Assimilation
Accommodation
Equilibration
How does learning happen?

When learners encounter something new, they try to fit it into existing schemas. If it fits, this is assimilation. If it doesn't fit, the schema must be restructured — this is accommodation. The tension between the two drives learning forward through a process called equilibration.

New Experience
Encounter something unfamiliar
→
Assimilation or Accommodation
Fit in or rebuild schema
→
Equilibrium
New understanding reached
Key Thinkers

Jean Piaget

Described how children progress through developmental stages of thinking, constructing knowledge through interaction with the environment.

John Dewey

"Learning by doing." Education should be rooted in real experience and reflection, not passive reception of facts.

Jerome Bruner

Advocated for discovery learning and the spiral curriculum — revisiting core ideas at increasing levels of complexity.

Key Concepts
Schema
Mental frameworks we use to organize and interpret new information.
Assimilation
Fitting new information into an existing schema without changing it.
Accommodation
Modifying an existing schema to account for new, conflicting information.
Equilibration
The drive to maintain balance between existing schemas and new experiences.
Discovery Learning
Students uncover principles themselves through exploration, not instruction.
Spiral Curriculum
Revisiting concepts repeatedly with increasing depth (Bruner).
Practical Tools for Your Classroom
🔍

Problem-based learning (PBL)

Present students with a real-world problem before teaching the solution. Let them explore, fail, and construct understanding before you provide input.

🧪

Activate prior knowledge

Start lessons by asking "What do you already know about this?" New knowledge sticks when connected to what students already have in their schemas.

📐

Project work & hands-on tasks

In vocational training, building, assembling, or solving a real task is the learning itself — not preparation for learning.

💬

Ask students to explain

When students articulate their understanding in their own words, they consolidate it. Use "explain to a partner" and "teach me how you solved that".

✓ Strengths

Promotes deep understanding, creativity, and transfer of knowledge. Learners who construct knowledge are better equipped to solve new problems.

✗ Limitations

Can feel unstructured. Without enough guidance, students may construct misconceptions. Requires careful facilitation.

✏ Reflect

When do your students build real understanding?

Think of a topic where students often struggle. Could you design a task where they explore the concept hands-on before you explain it? What would that look like in your subject area?

Theory 03 — Sociocultural Theory

Learning happens between people

Vygotsky argued that learning is fundamentally social. What a student can do with support today, they can do alone tomorrow. The teacher's role is to bridge that gap.

ZPD
Scaffolding
Mediation
MKO
How does learning happen?

Learning is embedded in social interaction, language, and cultural tools. The most important concept is the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) — the space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with expert support. The role of the teacher (or peer) is to provide temporary scaffolding within this zone.

What the student can achieve with support (ZPD)
Current Level
What they can do alone
Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development
Key Thinkers

Lev Vygotsky

Introduced ZPD and argued that language is the primary tool of thought. Learning precedes development.

Lave & Wenger

Developed the concept of situated learning and communities of practice — learning as participation in a social community.

James Wertsch

Extended Vygotsky's ideas to include how cultural tools (language, diagrams, technology) mediate thinking and learning.

Key Concepts
ZPD
The gap between independent ability and potential with guidance.
Scaffolding
Temporary support structures that are gradually removed as competence grows.
Mediation
Use of tools (language, diagrams, symbols) to bridge thinking and learning.
MKO
More Knowledgeable Other — the teacher, peer, or resource who provides support.
Situated Learning
Knowledge is embedded in the context in which it is used (Lave & Wenger).
Community of Practice
Learning through participation in a shared social community with shared goals.
Practical Tools for Your Classroom
🪜

Scaffolding & fading

Start with full support (worked examples, checklists, step-by-step guides), then systematically reduce help as confidence grows. Never remove scaffolding all at once.

🗣️

Think-alouds & modelling

Verbalize your own reasoning while demonstrating a task. "Now I notice the pressure is too high, so I'm going to adjust it because..." — language makes invisible expertise visible.

👥

Structured peer collaboration

Pair a more experienced student with a less experienced one on a real task. Explain clearly what each role should do. Learning through teaching is powerful.

💬

Rich classroom dialogue

Ask open questions that invite reasoning: "Why do you think that worked?" "What would happen if you tried it differently?" Language is the primary learning tool.

✓ Strengths

Highly relevant for vocational training. Explains mentorship, apprenticeship, and workplace learning. Recognizes the social nature of professional competence.

✗ Limitations

Requires well-structured collaboration. Without guidance, group work can be unproductive. Measuring ZPD precisely is difficult.

✏ Reflect

Where are you the "More Knowledgeable Other"?

Think about a skill your students are developing. What do they currently struggle with? How do you bridge the gap between where they are and where they need to be? Are you fading your support as they improve?

Theory 04 — Cognitivism

The mind as an information processor

Cognitivism looks inside the black box. Learning is about how the mind receives, processes, stores, and retrieves information. Understanding this helps you design lessons that actually stick.

Working Memory
Long-term Memory
Metacognition
Bloom's Taxonomy
How does learning happen?

New information enters through the senses into sensory register, then moves to working memory for active processing. If well-encoded, it transfers to long-term memory. The quality of learning depends on how deeply information is processed and how well it connects to existing knowledge.

Sensory Register
Brief, raw input
→
Working Memory
~7 items, active thinking
→
Long-term Memory
Unlimited, permanent
Atkinson & Shiffrin's Multi-Store Model of Memory
Key Thinkers

David Ausubel

Emphasized meaningful learning: new knowledge must connect to existing knowledge. Introduced advance organizers as a teaching tool.

Benjamin Bloom

Created Bloom's Taxonomy — a hierarchy of cognitive skills from remembering to creating. Used worldwide for lesson planning.

John Flavell

Coined metacognition — thinking about one's own thinking. Students who monitor their own understanding learn more effectively.

Key Concepts
Cognitive Load
Working memory has limited capacity. Overloading it blocks learning.
Meaningful Learning
New knowledge must link to what students already know (Ausubel).
Bloom's Taxonomy
6-level hierarchy: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create.
Metacognition
Awareness and regulation of one's own thinking and learning processes.
Advance Organizer
An overview or frame given before teaching to help students attach new knowledge.
Spaced Repetition
Reviewing material at increasing intervals improves long-term retention.
Bloom's Taxonomy in Practice

Use this when planning learning objectives. Move students from lower to higher cognitive levels over time.

Create — Design, produce, construct, compose
Evaluate — Judge, critique, justify, assess
Analyze — Compare, differentiate, organize, examine
Apply — Use, implement, solve, demonstrate
Understand — Explain, summarize, interpret, paraphrase
Remember — Recall, list, recognize, name
Practical Tools for Your Classroom
🗺️

Concept maps

Have students map the relationships between key ideas visually. This forces them to structure and connect knowledge — moving it deeper into long-term memory.

📌

Advance organizers

Before a new topic, show a visual overview or outline of what's coming. This gives students cognitive "hooks" to attach new information to.

🔄

Spaced repetition

Revisit key content after 1 day, 1 week, and 1 month. Brief retrieval practice (quiz, question) is far more effective than re-reading.

🧭

Teach metacognitive strategies

Help students monitor their own understanding: "Do I actually understand this, or do I just recognize it?" Teach them to self-test, not just re-read.

✓ Strengths

Offers powerful, evidence-based instructional strategies (spaced practice, retrieval practice, reducing cognitive load). Very practical for lesson design.

✗ Limitations

Can become overly technical. Doesn't fully account for emotional, social, or cultural factors in learning.

✏ Reflect

Are your students overloaded?

Think about a complex lesson you teach. How much new information do you introduce at once? Do students have the cognitive scaffolding to handle it? How could you use advance organizers or chunking to reduce cognitive load?

Apply & Compare

All four theories — side by side

No single theory explains learning completely. Expert teachers draw on all four — choosing the right approach for the right moment. Here's how to see the differences clearly, and how to apply them.

Behaviorism Constructivism Sociocultural Cognitivism
View of learning Change in observable behavior Active knowledge construction Social & cultural participation Information processing
The teacher is… Instructor & reinforcer Facilitator & guide Scaffolder & MKO Explainer & designer
The student is… Passive receiver Active constructor Social participant Active processor
Key tool Reinforcement, drill Projects, exploration Dialogue, collaboration Concept maps, Bloom's
Best for Safety habits, procedures Problem-solving, creativity Mentorship, teamwork Structured knowledge transfer
Key limitation Ignores the inner life Can lack structure Needs well-managed groups Misses social/emotional factors
Classroom Scenarios

Read each scenario. Think about which theory best explains the situation. Then reveal the analysis.

Scenario 1

The student who always forgets safety goggles

You have a student in your workshop who repeatedly forgets to put on safety goggles. You've reminded him many times. What theory guides your next move?

Behaviorist approach: The behavior (forgetting goggles) has not been consistently followed by an unpleasant consequence or extinguished. Implement a clear, consistent rule with immediate consequences every single time. Also consider using positive reinforcement: when he puts them on unprompted, acknowledge it specifically. Consistency is everything in operant conditioning.
Scenario 2

Students who can follow steps but can't problem-solve

Your students can follow a manual perfectly, but when something goes wrong they freeze. They've memorized the process but don't understand why it works.

Constructivist response: They've been trained, not educated. They've assimilated steps without building real schemas. Try deliberately breaking the procedure and asking students to diagnose what went wrong. Let them struggle productively before you explain. Introduce tasks with no single right answer. Their job is to build understanding, not just reproduce steps.
Scenario 3

The silent student who learns by watching

One of your students rarely speaks in class, but you notice she performs excellently when working alongside experienced peers. She struggles alone.

Sociocultural lens: She's learning within the ZPD — she can do things with support that she can't yet do alone. This is not a weakness; it's exactly where learning happens. Structure more collaborative tasks for her, ensure the pairing is supportive rather than dominant, and gradually give her tasks that require more independence. Track whether the gap narrows over time.
Scenario 4

Students who seem to understand but fail the exam

In class, everything seems fine — students nod, respond correctly. But test results are poor. What's going wrong?

Cognitivist diagnosis: Students are likely recognizing information rather than retrieving it — a classic illusion of knowing. They've been passive (re-reading, listening) instead of actively retrieving (self-testing, explaining from memory). Switch to frequent, low-stakes retrieval practice. Use exit tickets, mini-quizzes, and ask students to explain concepts with the notes closed. Spaced repetition over time will improve retention.
